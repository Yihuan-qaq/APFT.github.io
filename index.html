APFT: Adaptive Phoneme Filter Template to Generate Anti-compression Speech Adversarial Example in Real-time

Yihuan Huang1,2, Yanzhen Ren1,2, Member, IEEE, Zongkun Sun1,2, Liming Zhai3, Jingmin Wang1,2, Wuyang Liu1,2, Graduate Student Member, IEEE
1Key Laboratory of Aerospace Information Security and Trusted Computing, Ministry of Education 2School of Cyber Science and Engineering, Wuhan University 3School of Computer Science, Central China Normal University

Abstract

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems are extensively employed for speech censoring. Speech Adversarial Example (AE) offers a novel approach to protecting speech privacy by forcing ASR to mistranscribe. However, existing speech AE face two challenges in real-time voice interactive applications, such as IP telephone, voice chat, or video conference, it cannot be generated in real-time, and its defensive capability is significantly reduced after the essential audio compression for network transmission.

In this paper, the Adaptive Phoneme Filter Template (APFT) is proposed to solve these problems. The key features of APFT include: 1) Phoneme-level Template, which generates the universal AE templates for each phoneme, enabling APFT to generate AE in real-time. 2) Filter, which generates AE by eliminating redundant signals from the speech rather than by adding carefully crafted noise, improving the compression robustness of APFT. 3) Adaptive Band Filtering (ABF), which obtains the frequency bands related to the speech content and applies Phoneme-level Template and Filter in these bands to improve the speech quality.

The comprehensive experimental results show that APFT has four advantages: 1) Real-time. The average AE generation time of APFT for one second speech is lower than 1.1 ms. 2) Compression Robustness. The average WER of APFT achieves 0.64 under the popular audio codec AAC and Opus, while the comparative methods only achieve 0.24 and 0.15 . 3) Transferability. APFT is trained on a single dataset and ASR system. When evaluated on three additional datasets and four ASR systems, APFT achieved an average WER of 0.72, significantly outperforming the comparison methods, which achieved average WER of 0.29 and 0.12. 4) Stealthiness. The AE generated by APFT achieves a MOS of 4.07, indicating the high speech quality. Furthermore, to evaluate the performance of APFT in real-world applications, we deploye it into the voice call of Telegram which uses Opus as audio encoder. Experimental results show the practical applicability of APFT in real-time interaction scenarios. APFT raises the WER of the Google speech recognition model to 0.714, indicating its effectiveness in preventing the censorship of speech content in online interactive applications.
